Money and Desire

You were taught to suppress your desire. To call it ego. To dress your hunger in the language of service so nobody would think you were greedy. But your desire is not the problem. The suppression of it is.

Madalena Dragon Spirituality & Money 6 min read

There is something that happens to spiritual women and their relationship with money. A very specific kind of contortion.

They want money. They need money. Their mission requires money. But they have been taught that desire is dangerous, that wanting is a sign of ego, that a truly spiritual person should somehow float above the material realm and trust that abundance will simply arrive.

So they suppress the desire. They dress it up. They say they just want to serve. They charge prices that don't actually sustain their lives. They give away too much for free. They attract clients who want to be saved rather than clients who want to grow.

And underneath all of it, the desire is still there. Unexpressed. Unmet. And increasingly resentful.

Where This Comes From

The suppression of desire has a long history. Religious traditions that taught the body and its appetites were sinful. Family systems where wanting things was considered selfish or ungrateful. Cultural narratives that told women, in particular, that their value lay in what they gave, not what they received.

Add the spiritual layer, and you get someone who has a sophisticated framework for explaining why she should not want what she actually wants.

It's not spirituality. It is a very old wound dressed in spiritual language.

What Desire Actually Is

Desire is not ego. Desire is life force.

It is the same force that makes trees grow toward light, that makes rivers find the ocean, that makes a woman feel called to build something that did not exist before she arrived.

Your desire for money is not separate from your desire to create, to build, to contribute, to have freedom, to give your children a different life, to stop making decisions from scarcity. It is the same energy. The same impulse toward more life.

When you suppress desire, you suppress all of it. The passion for your work. The creative energy. The hunger that makes you magnetic. The life force that makes clients feel your conviction and say yes.

What Wanting Money Actually Means

Wanting money means you want options. You want to say yes to the things that matter and no to the things that don't. You want to show up to your work from fullness rather than desperation.

Wanting money means you want your mission to reach more people. A well-resourced mission goes further than a broke one. Always.

Wanting money means you want to stop choosing between your needs and the needs of the people you love. That is not greed. That is love.

The spiritual community has confused having money with being materialistic. They are not the same thing. You can be fully devoted to your path and also have a thriving, well-paid business. The two are not in conflict.

The Shift

The shift begins when you stop apologising for wanting.

When you say, clearly and without shame: I want to earn well. I want my business to grow. I want financial freedom. I want this mission to be funded.

Not because having money makes you better. Because having money means having a choice. And a woman with choices is a woman who can give from genuine abundance rather than depletion.

Your desire for money is not a distraction from your spiritual path. It is part of it.

Because the next stage of your evolution is not transcending the material world. It is learning to show up fully inside it.

The Practice

Stop shrinking your money goals. Write them down without qualifying them. Without adding "as long as it's for the right reasons" or "if it's in alignment." Just write what you actually want.

Notice how that feels in your body. Notice the contraction, the guilt, the voice that says you're being selfish. That is not your soul speaking. That is the conditioning.

Your desire is not the problem. It is the compass. It knows where you are supposed to go.

Let it lead.
